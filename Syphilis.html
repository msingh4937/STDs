Syphilis

Syphilis is a sexually transmitted infection that can cause serious health problems if it is not treated. Syphilis is divided into stages (primary, secondary, and tertiary).

Syphilis makes it much easier to get and give HIV. Many people don't notice syphilis symptoms or know they have syphilis.

Stages of Syphilis

Primary Stage

During Stage 1, painless sores called chancres will appear at the site of the infection The sores heal on their own after 3 to 6 weeks, but you can still spread syphilis. It is recommended to treat it to stop the infection from moving to stage 2.

Secondary Stage

During Stage 2, you will experience symptoms such as rough red or reddish brown rash on the palms of your hands and at the bottom of your feet, swollen lymph nodes, fever, sore throat, patchy hair loss, headaches, body aches, or fatigue.

Tertiary Stage

During Stage 3, symptoms from Stage 2 disappears and syphilis isn't contagious, but the infection has started affcting your organs which could leadto death. Symptoms in Stage 3 includes problems controlling muscle movements, numbness, vision problems that can lead to blindness, and dementia.

Syphilis

Syphilis cannot be spread by toilet seats, door knobs, swimming pools, hot tubs, bath tubs, shared clothing, or eating utensils.

Syphilis is a curable disease with prompt diagnosis and treatment. However, if treated too late, there may be permanent damage to the heart and brain even after the infection is destroyed.


Syphilis Facts

Syphilis is one of the 10 most common STDs. Syphilis is caused by the bacterium Treponema pallidum. Syphilis is treated with a penicillin injection.Syphilis can spread from an infected mother to her unborn baby. Any sexually active person can get syphilis through unprotected vaginal, anal, or oral sex. Have an honest and open talk with your health care provider and ask whether you should be tested for syphilis or other STDs.

Treponema Pallidum

Treponema pallidum is the causative organism of syphilis. It is a motile spirochete that is generally acquired by close sexual contact and which enters host tissue by breaches in squamous or columnar epithelium.
